Jensen Huang’s distinction between task and purpose is one of the more useful ways to talk about AI and work. Every job contains tasks, but it also contains a purpose. AI can take over more and more of the task layer without taking over the reason the job exists.

That is a better starting point than the usual lazy question of which jobs AI will “kill.” Jobs are bundles of activities, decisions, accountabilities, relationships, and institutional trust. Once you look at work that way, the real issue is not whether AI can imitate visible activity. It is whether a role still has enough judgment, accountability, and decision rights left after the activity gets cheaper.

## The useful distinction

A radiologist does not exist merely to inspect images. A lawyer does not exist merely to draft text. A software engineer does not exist merely to type code.

Those are tasks. The purpose sits above them:

- diagnose disease
- protect a client’s interests
- solve a business problem
- reduce risk
- create an outcome someone is willing to pay for

This matters because AI and automation usually attack the most legible part of work first: the part that can be scoped, repeated, and evaluated locally. That is why so many current AI use cases cluster around summarising, drafting, extracting, classifying, reformatting, and producing first-pass analysis.

## Where AI bites first

AI is strongest where work is narrow, repeatable, and easy to evaluate at the task level. In practice, that includes things like:

- summarising reports
- generating first-draft code
- extracting clauses from contracts
- categorising tickets
- reformatting data
- drafting routine communications
- producing standard analysis

OECD research points in the same direction. In occupations with high AI exposure, the skills that remain most demanded are not only technical production skills, but also management, business-process understanding, coordination, and social capability. The value does not disappear. It shifts upward from execution alone to orchestration, judgment, and ownership.

## Task-heavy versus purpose-anchored roles

“Safe” versus “unsafe” is the wrong frame. “Task-heavy” versus “purpose-anchored” is more useful.

A task-heavy role is one where the worker mainly performs bounded activities inside a workflow designed by someone else. The tasks may still be skilled, but the role has limited authority over goals, trade-offs, or outcomes.

Common examples:

- the analyst whose real job is producing decks, not shaping decisions
- the developer who mainly converts tickets into boilerplate code
- the paralegal doing first-pass extraction and formatting
- the service agent following scripts, completing forms, and routing issues
- the junior project role that mostly updates trackers, chases inputs, and assembles status packs

A purpose-anchored role looks different. Here the worker is paid not mainly to perform steps, but to own an outcome under real-world constraints.

Examples:

- the architect who helps the organisation make better technology decisions and live with the consequences
- the doctor who must diagnose, explain, and decide
- the lawyer who exercises judgment, advocacy, and fiduciary responsibility
- the product manager who decides what matters and why
- the executive who sets direction, allocates attention, and carries accountability

AI may compress large parts of the task layer in all of these roles, but that does not remove the purpose. In many cases, it makes the purpose more exposed.

## The uncomfortable implication

When AI takes over a large share of the visible work, it reveals what the role was really for all along.

If someone cannot explain the problem they own beyond the tasks they perform, they are more exposed than they think. Statements like these are weak descriptions of value:

- “I produce reports.”
- “I write code.”
- “I prepare material.”
- “I review documents.”

These describe activity, not purpose. In an AI-heavy environment, activity is the first thing to become cheap.

A better test is whether you can answer questions like:

1. What outcome do I own?
2. What trade-offs am I trusted to make?
3. What risk do I reduce or absorb?
4. Who depends on my judgment, not just my output?
5. What still requires me when the first draft is free?

## Where the framing can mislead

This distinction is useful, but it can also become a comforting slogan.

It is not enough to say that AI cannot automate purpose. In some roles, what people call “purpose” is just a thin wrapper around a bundle of routine tasks. Once the workflow is redesigned, much of the role can disappear anyway.

That is why the more cautious labour-market research matters. Brookings has argued that the evidence on AI and labour demand is still early and inconclusive, and that the larger effects may come from workflow redesign rather than from a simple one-for-one replacement of workers by tools.

That is the real test: not whether purpose survives in the abstract, but whether a role has enough genuine judgment, accountability, trust, and decision rights to remain valuable after the task layer is compressed.

Some roles do. Some roles do not. Many roles will likely split in two:

- a smaller, more demanding purpose-anchored core
- AI systems and lighter-weight roles around it that absorb much of the surrounding task execution

## Practical implications

For individuals, the strategic move is not to defend yesterday’s task list. It is to move up a layer.

That means becoming the person who:

- frames the problem
- interprets ambiguity
- makes trade-offs explicit
- aligns stakeholders
- earns trust
- carries the consequences of decisions

For leaders, the question is slightly different. Do not ask only where AI can save effort. Ask what happens to role design when the effort layer collapses.

A useful review checklist is:

1. Separate the purpose of each role from its current task bundle.
2. Identify which tasks are compressible now, not in theory.
3. Redesign workflows before redesigning headcount.
4. Protect decision quality, accountability, and trust handoffs.
5. Retrain people toward ownership, coordination, and judgment.

The cleanest way to put it is this: AI does not care much about your tasks, except as something to compress. What it tests is whether your work has a purpose strong enough to survive once the old effort disappears.

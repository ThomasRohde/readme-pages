Most knowledge workers will not say it out loud. But the honest ones, if pressed, will admit something like this: I do not think anymore. I go straight to the best LLM available and prompt. They are way smarter than I am. But I get the original thought still.

That admission contains three distinct claims, and each carries different weight. The first — that analytical processing has been delegated to machines — is empirically measurable and increasingly well-supported. The second — that the machines are better at it — is harder to contest with every model generation. The third — that something irreducible remains with the human — is the philosophically interesting one.

If the third claim holds, it radically redefines what it means to be a knowledge worker. The value shifts from the ability to reason, analyse, and articulate to something upstream: the generative act of seeing what matters, naming the right problem, and framing the question that unlocks a useful answer.

## Where does the mind stop?

Andy Clark and David Chalmers asked this question in 1998 with their Extended Mind Thesis. Their answer: cognitive processes are not confined to the skull. If an external tool performs a function we would recognise as mental if done internally, it is part of the cognitive process. Otto's notebook is part of Otto's memory.

Clark updated this framework for AI in a 2025 paper in *Nature Communications*, arguing that humans are fundamentally "hybrid thinking systems" that incorporate non-biological resources. This is not a deviation from our nature — it *is* our nature. LLMs are the latest, most powerful cognitive extension.

But Clark adds an important qualifier: **cognitive hygiene** — the metacognitive discipline required to use AI well. You must understand how the system fails, assess reliability, and maintain evaluative engagement. Extension without hygiene is not cognition. It is abdication.

Chloe Kitzinger at Columbia pushes back harder. She argues that previous cognitive tools — writing, calculators, search engines — each externalised a specific human function while leaving the rest intact. LLMs externalise the entire reasoning chain, from problem decomposition through to conclusion. The Extended Mind Thesis was designed for notebooks, not for systems that generate novel arguments from statistical patterns. The framework may be too accommodating.

## What the brain scans show

The MIT Media Lab's 2025 study "Your Brain on ChatGPT" provides the most striking empirical evidence. Researchers measured neural connectivity in participants who wrote essays with LLM assistance, search engine assistance, or unaided.

Brain connectivity systematically scaled down with external support. Brain-only participants showed the strongest, widest-ranging neural networks. LLM users showed the weakest. More troublingly, the effect persisted: participants who used LLMs for three sessions and then switched to brain-only showed weaker neural connectivity than those who started unaided. The cognitive atrophy was not situational. It carried over.

The researchers coined the term **cognitive debt** — like technical debt, it accumulates silently and compounds. Each delegation of thinking feels costless in the moment. The cumulative effect is erosion.

A separate CHI 2025 study by Microsoft Research and Carnegie Mellon surveyed 319 knowledge workers across 936 examples of AI use. Between 69 and 79 percent reported decreased cognitive effort across all levels of Bloom's taxonomy — from basic recall to evaluation. Confidence in AI inversely correlated with critical thinking: the more you trust it, the less you interrogate it.

The study identifies a telling ambiguity: does decreased effort mean the same thinking with better support, or less thinking altogether? The workers could not reliably distinguish between the two. If you cannot tell whether you are thinking less, you probably are.

## The forklift in the weight room

Ted Chiang provides the sharpest counter-argument. In his 2024 *New Yorker* essay and 2025 Princeton lecture:

> Using ChatGPT to write your essays is like bringing a forklift into the weight room. You are never going to improve your cognitive fitness that way.

Chiang's point is not that AI output is bad. It is that the *process* of thinking through a problem is itself valuable — not just instrumentally, but constitutively. Writing forces you to organise thought. Analysis forces you to confront contradictions. Synthesis forces you to make choices at every level of resolution. Delegating these to AI delegates the very activities that develop cognitive capacity.

This cuts directly at the claim "but I get the original thought still." Chiang would say: the original thought is thin. A question without the sustained, difficult, iterative work of answering it is a starting point, not a contribution. The choices at every level — word, sentence, argument, structure — are where thinking actually happens. Compressing that into a prompt discards the essential part.

## But the question is not trivial

The counter-counter is equally serious.

Lazovsky et al., writing in the *Journal of Creative Behavior* in 2025, make the academic case that question-asking and prompt engineering share a "symbiotic relationship" grounded in creativity, critical thinking, and cognitive flexibility. Their most important finding: it may be more important to develop skills in crafting descriptions of problems than in mastering prompt engineering. The cognitive work of defining the problem is harder and more valuable than optimising the wording.

John Vervaeke at the University of Toronto argues that **problem-finding** — the capacity to identify, formulate, and frame problems worth solving — is central to wisdom and is cognitively superior to problem-solving. His concept of relevance realisation — the ability to determine what matters in a given context — highlights something AI does conspicuously poorly. AI generates; it does not determine relevance. That remains a human act.

Socrates did not write. He asked questions. His philosophical contribution was not the answers — he disclaimed having any — but the quality of the inquiry itself. There is something Socratic about the claim "I get the original thought still." The value is not in the analytical processing. It is in seeing what needs to be processed.

Herbert Simon's bounded rationality framework survives this transition intact, but with an inversion. Simon argued that humans satisfice because our cognitive resources are bounded — we stop at good enough because we cannot process everything. AI appears to remove those bounds. But the bounds do not disappear. They shift. The new bottleneck is not processing capacity but direction: the ability to aim AI at the right problem, evaluate its output, and decide what is worth pursuing.

## Taste as the last differentiator

A growing body of work argues that in a world of abundant AI execution, **taste** — the judgment to know what is worth doing — becomes the scarce, irreplaceable human capability.

Paul Graham captured it in 2002: the recipe for great work is exacting taste, plus the ability to gratify it. AI now provides the ability to gratify. Taste remains the human contribution.

Harvard Business School research by Rembrand Koning tested this empirically. In a study of 640 entrepreneurs given access to GPT-4, high-performing entrepreneurs saw profits increase by 10 to 15 percent. Low-performing entrepreneurs experienced approximately 8 percent decreases. The difference was not the AI — both groups got the same tool. The difference was selective judgment: the ability to evaluate, filter, and contextualise AI output. Without that judgment, AI access was not neutral. It was harmful.

The AI amplified existing capability gaps rather than closing them. This is perhaps the most important empirical finding in the entire debate.

## The central tension

The literature reveals two positions that resist easy synthesis.

The **extension view** (Clark, Mollick, the taste theorists): AI extends human cognition. The human contribution shifts upstream to framing, judgment, and direction. This is a natural evolution of our hybrid cognitive nature. The original thought is the genuinely hard part, and retaining it while delegating analytical processing is exactly how hybrid intelligence should work.

The **atrophy view** (Chiang, Carr, Han, the MIT team): Cognitive delegation accumulates debt. The process of thinking through problems is itself valuable — not just instrumentally but constitutively. Outsourcing that process does not free up capacity; it atrophies it. The original thought is real but insufficient. Without the discipline of working through the answer, the capacity to generate original thoughts will itself degrade over time.

Byung-Chul Han, the Korean-German philosopher who won the 2025 Princess of Asturias Award, puts it starkly: we search for more information without gaining any real knowledge. We communicate constantly without participating in a community. We save masses of data without keeping track of our memories. The worry is not that AI produces bad output. It is that abundant output without effortful engagement produces nothing at all.

## The expertise prerequisite

The most useful resolution is contextual. Whether cognitive delegation is beneficial or harmful depends on the purpose — and on who is doing the delegating.

When the goal is production — a strategy document, an analysis, a decision brief — delegating analytical processing while retaining the original framing is legitimate and often superior. For a practitioner who has already developed deep expertise through years of effortful thinking, this is a reasonable use of accumulated cognitive capital. You earned the right to direct because you once did the work.

When the goal is development — building new understanding, developing expertise in an unfamiliar domain — delegation is dangerous. Chiang's forklift metaphor applies. The MIT cognitive debt data applies. If you delegate the analytical processing before you have developed the capacity to do it yourself, you are not extending your mind. You are preventing its development.

This suggests an uncomfortable implication. The claim "but I get the original thought" is only defensible for people who have already earned their judgment through years of unassisted thinking. The original thought is not conjured from nothing. It is the product of accumulated expertise, pattern recognition, and hard-won understanding of a domain. An expert who delegates to AI is extending cognition that already exists. A novice who delegates to AI is outsourcing cognition that never formed.

The HBS judgment gap data confirms this directly. Experts gained from AI access. Novices lost.

## What remains

The knowledge worker of 2026 is being redefined. Not as someone who holds domain knowledge — AI can access it instantly. Not as someone who performs analysis — AI does it faster. But as someone who identifies what matters, exercises judgment over AI output, takes responsibility for the decision, and — crucially — maintains the cognitive capital that makes original thought possible in the first place.

The first three can be done by someone who does not think anymore. The fourth cannot. It requires ongoing, deliberate, sometimes inefficient engagement with hard problems.

That is the paradox of cognitive delegation. The moment it becomes total, it undermines the capacity that makes it productive. The original thought is real. It is valuable. It may be the most important thing. But it depends on cognitive infrastructure that was built through the very analytical work now being delegated.

The practical question is not whether to delegate. That ship has sailed for most of us. The question is whether to maintain enough unassisted engagement — not for every task, not even for most tasks, but for enough — that the capacity to see what matters does not silently atrophy while we are busy prompting.
